Start with the Problem

A guide for everyone thinking about AI at work

Start with
the problem.

"

These are all reasonable instincts.
But the best results come from getting clear on what we're trying to fix first.

Think of it like going to the doctor

Two patients walk
into a doctor's office.

Patient A

"I need an MRI, some antibiotics, and probably surgery on my left knee."

vs

Patient B

"I've had a dull pain in my leg for three weeks. It gets worse on stairs. I can't run anymore."

A doctor never prescribes before diagnosing. They ask questions, understand the pain, then decide what to do. The clearer the description of what's wrong, the better the help, whatever shape that help takes.

Before you mention any tool

Three questions
that matter.

01

What is the actual pain?

When someone arrives with an idea for what to build, the most useful thing is to step back and describe what's going wrong in the first place.

✕"We need a way to summarise meetings faster"
✓"Tell me more — who's doing the summarising now, when does it slow them down, and what would 'faster' actually mean for them?"
02

Who feels it, and how badly?

One person having a bad day is different from a hundred people losing an hour every week. Both can be worth fixing — but not with the same time and effort.

Name the person · How often · What it costs them
03

What would better look like?

If this were fixed, what would feel different day-to-day? And is there a version of this that wouldn't need doing at all? It's also how we'll know later whether the fix has worked.

Choosing the right tool

When is AI
the right tool?

Once the problem is clear, you can think about how to solve it. Sometimes a simple rule does the job better, faster and cheaper than AI ever could. Here's how to tell them apart.

Here's the test

Could you write step-by-step instructions
that would work every single time,
even for the tricky cases?

Yes → A simple rule will do

Some tasks just need a reliable rule that runs the same way every time. That's automation, not AI. Faster to build, cheaper to run, often the better choice.

No → That's where AI shines

When a task involves judgment, context, or too much variation to spell out in advance, that's where AI is brilliant.

Send a welcome email when someone joins the team
same every timeAutomation
Write a welcome message that fits the person's role and team
needs judgmentAI
Move a support ticket tagged "billing" to the billing queue
same every timeAutomation
Spot that an angry customer needs a human to step in, fast
needs judgmentAI
Send a weekly report to the team every Monday at 9am
same every timeAutomation
Read that report and flag what actually needs attention this week
needs judgmentAI

Take this with you

How to describe
what you need.

Who's affected?

Our sales team.

What's going wrong?

They spend two hours before every pitch pulling info from four different systems, and still walk in unprepared half the time.

What would fixed look like?

Less prep time, no more chasing each other for the same documents every week.

Once you can answer those three, the conversation gets a lot more useful, whether that's with us, your team, or just yourself working it out on paper.

The takeaway

The best AI projects
don't start with AI.

They start with someone saying what's going wrong, who it affects, and what better would look like. Sometimes better means doing the same thing faster. Sometimes it means doing things that weren't possible before.

Start with the problem.
Everything else follows.
